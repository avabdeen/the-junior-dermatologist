Why do teenagers actually get pimples, and why is popping them a medical disaster? To understand the pathology of acne vulgaris, we must first analyze the microscopic anatomy of the skin ecosystem.

1. Anatomy of the Pilosebaceous Unit

A pore is not just a hole in the skin, it is the opening of a complex structure known as the pilosebaceous unit! This unit consists of a small hair follicle tunnel, that starts at the surface layer (the epidermis) and reaches down into the deeper layer (the dermis). Tucked inside this follicle is the sebaceous gland, soirt of like a bunny in a burrow. The normal biological role of this gland is to produce sebum, a waxy lipid mixture designed to waterproof, moisturize, and protect our outer skin layers.

2. Causes of acne

Acne, or more medically known as acne vulgaris, is a common inflammatory skin condition. Acne vulgaris is characterised by the formation of skin lesions that develop due to increased sebum production, hormonal fluctuations, and pore blockages.One of the most significant causes of acne is increased sebum production (the oily feeling you may get when you touch your skin) in hair follicles. That leaves a further question: what causes higher sebum production?

Androgens, such as testosterone and dihydrotestosterone, are found in both male and female counterparts, and increase by a marginal scale during puberty. The hormones bind to the sebaceous glands' receptors and increase in size and secretory activity. The spectrum of gland sensitivity is genetically different from person to person, which is why some individuals have more/less acne than others. Another contributor to oil production is stress. Many studies have shown a link between stress and acne formation. Doctors came to this conclusion as when cortisol is released, sebaceous gland activity increases and causes the worsening of acne.
